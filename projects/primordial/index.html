Primordial Emergent Behaviors & Neuroevolution

No gradient descent. No reward engineering. Just physics, mutation, and survival.

Scroll to explore

The Premise

What happens when you let evolution design both the body and the brain?

Most machine learning optimizes a fixed architecture toward a fixed objective. A human picks the network topology. A human crafts the loss function. Gradient descent handles the rest. This project throws all of that away and lets evolution do the work from scratch.

Primordial is an artificial life simulation. Organisms are dropped into a 2D world with food resources and nothing else. They have bodies made of springs and masses, brains made of neural networks, and both are encoded in a genome that mutates when they reproduce. The only optimization signal is survival: organisms that find food live long enough to reproduce. Those that don't, die. Their bodies become food for others.

Over thousands of generations, organisms evolve bodies with sensors pointed toward food. They evolve gaits, periodic muscle contraction patterns that produce directed locomotion. They evolve feeding strategies. Species diverge. Ecosystems form. None of this is programmed.

Chapter I

Genesis

The simulation begins with 120 organisms scattered across a landscape. Each has a random neural network and a simple five-node body: a core, a sensor, two muscle anchors, and a mouth. Their brains fire random signals. Muscles twitch without purpose. Most organisms wander aimlessly, burning energy faster than they can find food.

But a few, by pure chance, have neural weights that produce net movement toward nearby plant resources. These organisms eat. They survive. Everything that follows descends from them.

Chapter II

First Divergence

Within hundreds of ticks, mutations accumulate. A child's genome diverges far enough from its parent that the speciation threshold is crossed and a new species is born. This first split is modest: the two lineages are nearly identical. But it marks the beginning of an evolutionary tree that will branch dozens of times.

Each new species represents a slightly different strategy encoded in slightly different neural weights and body configurations. Some add extra sensor nodes. Others shift muscle positions for better locomotion. The genome is the blueprint, and natural selection is the architect.

Chapter III

The Bloom

Once organisms evolve effective foraging behaviors, the population explodes. Successful neural network configurations are inherited by offspring, who are born pre-wired to seek food. The population climbs rapidly as reproduction outpaces death. Resources become scarce. Competition intensifies.

This is the first Malthusian crisis: a population that has learned to exploit its environment now risks exhausting it. The carrying capacity of the world becomes the dominant selective pressure.

The Bloom — Population Explosion

Successful strategies propagate rapidly. Watch the population counter climb as organisms reproduce faster than they die.

Chapter IV

Radiation

As different regions of the world offer different resource densities and competitive pressures, species diversify into distinct niches. Some lineages evolve larger bodies with more sensors. Others stay small and energy-efficient. Body plans vary from compact three-node sprinters to sprawling multi-sensor foragers.

This is adaptive radiation: a single ancestral population branching into multiple species, each specialized for slightly different conditions. The ecosystem reaches its peak diversity, with dozens of species coexisting simultaneously.

Radiation — Peak Species Diversity

Chapter V

Collapse

Overpopulation depletes resources. Food becomes scarce. Organisms that were thriving now starve. The population crashes, sometimes losing half its numbers in a few hundred ticks. Entire species go extinct. The survivors are those with the most efficient metabolisms, the best foraging strategies, or simply the luckiest positions when resources respawn.

Population crashes are a recurring pattern in the simulation: boom, overshoot, bust, recovery. Each crash is a bottleneck that reshapes the gene pool, eliminating weaker lineages and concentrating the genome around survival-tested configurations.

Chapter VI

Balance

After the initial cycles of boom and bust, the ecosystem settles toward a dynamic equilibrium. Population oscillates around the environment's carrying capacity. Species that have survived multiple crashes are well-adapted: efficient foragers with lean body plans and neural networks refined across many generations of selection.

The equilibrium is not static. New species still emerge from mutation. Old species still go extinct when outcompeted. But the wild swings of early evolution give way to subtler dynamics: gradual niche shifts, slow optimization of existing strategies, and the occasional disruptive innovation that reshuffles the competitive landscape.

Chapter VII

Deep Time

Tens of thousands of ticks in, organisms have inherited and refined their neural networks across dozens of generations. The genomes circulating in the population are the product of thousands of selection events. Bodies have accumulated mutations that survived: extra sensors, repositioned muscles, armor nodes on lineages that face competition.

These late-generation organisms are qualitatively different from the founding population. Their locomotion is smoother. Their foraging is more directed. Their bodies are more complex. None of this was programmed. It emerged from the same simple rules that started the simulation: physics, mutation, and survival.

The Body

Spring-mass morphology that evolves

Each organism's body is a graph of point masses connected by springs. The genome encodes node types and edge connections. Nodes come in seven varieties: a core that serves as the center of mass, bone nodes for structural rigidity, muscle anchors that form the endpoints of controllable springs, sensors that detect nearby food and organisms, mouths that eat, fat nodes that store energy, and armor nodes that reduce damage.

Edges are springs governed by Hooke's law. Bone springs are rigid with high stiffness and low damping. Muscle springs have a controllable rest length: the neural network sets a contraction target each tick, and the spring force drives the body toward that configuration. Tendon springs are semi-rigid connectors. The physics runs as Verlet integration with multiple substeps per simulation tick.

Locomotion is discovered, not programmed. An organism with muscles on its sides has to learn to alternate contractions to produce forward movement. Asymmetric bodies produce turning. Bodies with sensors at the front and muscles at the back evolve to chase food. The relationship between morphology and behavior is entirely emergent.

The Brain

Neural networks optimized by survival, not gradients

Each organism has a feedforward neural network. The input layer receives sensory data: distance and type of nearest food and organisms per sensor node, the organism's energy level and age, and proprioceptive feedback from current muscle lengths. A small memory register persists values across ticks, giving organisms a primitive form of short-term memory.

The output layer controls the body. Each muscle gets a contraction target. Two additional outputs signal eating intent and reproductive readiness. The hidden layer size, activation function, and all weights are encoded in the genome.

There is no backpropagation and no loss function. Weights are inherited from parents with Gaussian noise, and the mutation scale itself is a genome parameter that evolves. Organisms that survive long enough to reproduce pass their weights to offspring. A child is born pre-wired with its parent's learned behaviors plus small random variations. Over generations, the population converges on effective control strategies through pure selection pressure.

The Mechanism

Mutation, speciation, and meta-evolution

Reproduction is energy-gated. An organism must accumulate enough energy (60% of capacity for asexual, 50% for sexual) and survive long enough (100 ticks minimum) before it can split. The offspring inherits a mutated copy of the parent's genome. Body mutations add or remove nodes, change node types, add or remove edges, and perturb node positions. An evolvable symmetry bias makes mirrored mutations more likely, producing bilaterally symmetric creatures.

Brain mutations perturb weights, toggle connections, and occasionally change the hidden layer size. Larger bodies cost more energy to maintain and more energy to reproduce, creating natural pressure against unbounded growth.

Speciation emerges from a genome distance metric. When a child's genome diverges past a threshold from its parent, it's assigned a new species ID. This enables tracking of evolutionary lineages and prevents incompatible genomes from sexually reproducing.

Mutation rates are themselves genome parameters. An organism with a high body mutation rate produces more varied offspring. Some will be worse, but some might discover advantageous new morphologies. An organism with a low mutation rate produces more consistent offspring, preserving what works. Evolution optimizes its own rate of exploration.

The Ecosystem

Food chains that nobody designed

The environment is a continuous 2D world with toroidal topology (edges wrap). Plant resources spawn over time with a bias toward nutrient-rich areas. When organisms die, their body mass converts to meat resources that decay slowly. Decayed meat produces nutrients that boost local plant growth. This creates a complete energy cycle: sun to plants to herbivores to carnivores to decomposition to nutrients to plants.

Organisms with mouth nodes can eat plants by contact. They can also bite other organisms: mouth-on-body contact deals damage proportional to the number of mouths, reduced by the target's armor nodes. If hunting other organisms is more energy-efficient than foraging for plants, evolution will find that strategy. Carnivory, herbivory, and scavenging are emergent strategies, not programmed categories.

Anti-extinction safeguards prevent total ecosystem collapse: plant spawn rate never drops to zero, and if the population crashes below a threshold, random viable organisms immigrate into the world. These mechanisms allow dramatic population dynamics (booms, busts, occasional mass extinction followed by rapid diversification) without the simulation going permanently dead.

System

What emerges

Over 30,000 simulation ticks, a founding population of 120 organisms diversifies into dozens of species across many generations. Bodies accumulate new nodes as morphological mutations that improve survival propagate through the gene pool. Population dynamics oscillate around the environment's carrying capacity, with boom-bust cycles driven by resource competition and speciation events reshaping the ecosystem.

The simulation engine processes 18-40 ticks per second depending on population size, using NumPy-vectorized spring physics and spatial hash grids for O(1) neighbor queries. State snapshots are recorded at key narrative moments for D3.js replay visualization.
