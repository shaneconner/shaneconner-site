The Project

Artificial life through evolutionary computation

Primordial is an evolutionary life simulation. Organisms have spring-mass bodies with evolvable morphology and feedforward neural networks that control locomotion, feeding, and reproduction. There is no loss function, no gradient descent, and no reward shaping. Organisms that accumulate enough energy reproduce. Those that don't, die. Everything else — body shape, behavior, predation, cooperation, speciation — emerges from the physics.

The simulation runs in a continuous 2D world with toroidal topology. Plant resources spawn over time, dead organisms decay into meat, and decayed matter fertilizes new plant growth. Bodies are graphs of point masses connected by springs: bones for structure, muscles for locomotion, sensors for perception, mouths for feeding, fat for energy storage, armor for defense. Each node type has a metabolic cost, creating natural pressure for efficient design.

Primordial is a multi-part investigation. Each part explores a different set of rules and constraints, revealing how small changes to the simulation's mechanics produce dramatically different evolutionary outcomes. The question isn't what organisms can evolve — it's what they will evolve, given the rules of the world they inhabit.

The Investigation

Two experiments, two outcomes

Part 1

Emergent Behaviors & Neuroevolution

The first run reveals what happens when evolution is given free rein with abundant resources and simple senses. 100,000 ticks produce 128 generations, radiation-consolidation cycles, and a surprising convergence: every organism evolves toward the same minimal body plan. The simulation discovers that simplicity beats complexity when the rules don't reward size.

100k ticks 128 generations 4,345 events

Part 2

Fixing the Rules

Seven changes to the simulation mechanics. Larger bodies, richer sensors, separate eat and attack signals, scarcer resources, sexual reproduction, longer lifespans, and mechanical advantages for complexity. The result: mass extinctions, fragile recoveries, boom-bust species cycles, and an ecosystem that never reaches equilibrium.
