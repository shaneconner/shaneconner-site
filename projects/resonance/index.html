The Problem

Streaming algorithms are optimizing for the wrong thing

Spotify's algorithm is optimized to keep you listening, not to deepen your understanding of what you actually like. It feeds you adjacencies to what you've already consumed, reinforcing a narrowing corridor of taste. Over time, the bubble tightens. Artists you loved three years ago disappear from rotation. Genres you explored once never resurface. Your listening history exists only as a stream of play counts with no structure, no context, and no memory of why you were drawn to something.

The deeper problem is fragmentation. Your taste profile is scattered across half a dozen platforms: Spotify has play history, Last.fm has scrobbles, MusicBrainz has metadata, Discogs has pressings and lineage, Genius has lyrics and annotations. Each platform holds a shard of understanding, but none of them talk to each other, and none of them model the relationships between artists, genres, and tracks in a way that evolves with your behavior.

This project is built on a different premise: what if your music library was a knowledge graph that learned from how you listen?

The Architecture

Seven APIs feed one unified schema

Spotify is the spine — tracks, albums, artists, all keyed on Spotify IDs. From there, six other sources feed in. Last.fm brings listening history and artist similarity. MusicBrainz adds authoritative metadata and recording relationships. ListenBrainz contributes community listening data. Deezer has audio previews and its own recommendation graph. Discogs has physical release lineage. Genius has lyrics.

Everything collapses into a single SQLite database: 27 tables, designed so any entity can be enriched from any source without duplication. A rate limiter keeps seven APIs happy simultaneously. Cross-referencing happens automatically through external ID mapping.

The result: every track exists in context. Its genre lineage. Its artist's collaborators. Its acoustic neighbors. How often you listen to it and where you found it.

Genre Structure

Genres don't exist in isolation

Music genre taxonomies are notoriously messy. Spotify assigns genres to artists, not tracks. Last.fm uses crowd-sourced tags that drift over time. MusicBrainz has its own genre vocabulary. Rather than picking one canonical system, this project builds a co-occurrence network: genres are connected when they appear together on shared artists.

The result is a graph that captures real relationships: post-punk and darkwave cluster together. Math rock bridges prog and indie. Electronic music splinters into dozens of micro-communities connected by shared ancestry. The topology of this network reveals more about genre relationships than any hierarchical taxonomy could.

Node size reflects track count. Edge weight reflects co-occurrence frequency. Color groups genres by community detection. The network is interactive — drag nodes to explore neighborhoods, hover for details.

The Mechanism

Listening frequency evolves from behavior

Every track, artist, and genre in the system has a frequency: a target interval in days between listens. This isn't a static configuration. It's a living value that evolves based on your actual listening behavior, using the same golden-ratio mechanism from the task intelligence system.

Listen to a track ahead of schedule? Its frequency decreases; the system surfaces it more often. Let a track go overdue? Its frequency increases; the system lets it drift further into the background. The multiplier is the golden ratio: 1.382. Symmetric and bounded so cadence adapts quickly without becoming unstable.

Over time, this creates a natural rotation. Tracks you actively seek out earn tighter intervals. Tracks you passively ignore gradually fade. The system doesn't decide what you should listen to; it learns what you do listen to and adapts accordingly. Survival of the chosen.

Rather than force one taxonomy, the system normalizes sub-genres into broader families and counts track associations. The result is a structural view of the library: where the mass is concentrated, what the long tail looks like, and how niche genres relate to broader categories.

Discovery

Recommendations from owned data, not rented algorithms

The discovery engine aggregates signals from five independent strategies. Last.fm provides similar-track and similar-artist recommendations anchored in scrobble data. Deezer contributes its own related-artist graph. ListenBrainz offers collaborative filtering from its open listening community. Genre exploration surfaces underexplored corners of the library's genre network. And Spotify's own catalog, seeded from the frequency model's most overdue artists, surfaces top tracks from artists the system knows you should revisit.

Each strategy runs independently, then results merge and rank by multi-source agreement. An artist recommended by both Last.fm similarity and Deezer related-artist graphs ranks higher than one from a single source. The system queues discoveries for review: add them to the library or dismiss them, and that feedback refines future recommendations.

The key difference from streaming platform recommendations: you own the graph. Discovery provenance is logged. You can see exactly why something was recommended, which sources agreed, and how it relates to what you already know.

Rotation State

The library as a living system

Every artist in the system has a target frequency and a current cycle progress: the ratio of days since last listened to the target frequency. When cycle progress exceeds 1.0, the artist is overdue. The scatter plot below shows each artist positioned by their target frequency (x-axis) and current cycle progress (y-axis). Points above the threshold line are overdue for a listen.

This is the system's pulse. At any given moment, it shows which artists are due, which are in comfortable rotation, and which have drifted to long intervals. The frequency model evolves continuously: listen ahead of schedule and the interval tightens; let something go overdue and the interval relaxes.

Audio Analysis

Acoustic neighborhoods that transcend genre labels

Genre labels are human shorthand: useful but lossy. Two tracks labeled "electronic" can sound completely different. Two tracks from different genres can share deep acoustic properties: tempo, harmonic content, spectral texture, rhythmic density. Audio feature extraction captures what genre labels approximate.

The system uses CLAP (Contrastive Language-Audio Pretraining) to generate 512-dimensional embeddings from Deezer 30-second audio previews. Each track becomes a point in a high-dimensional space where proximity means acoustic similarity. The t-SNE projection below collapses those 512 dimensions into two, revealing clusters of tracks that sound alike regardless of how they're tagged. Cosine similarity in CLAP space powers the audio similarity search: find tracks that share acoustic DNA across genre boundaries.

Prediction

Frequency-first, ML-enhanced

The system's primary ranking signal is cycle progress: how overdue something is relative to its evolved frequency. This works from day one and improves continuously as frequencies adapt. Tracks you seek out earn tighter intervals; tracks you ignore gradually relax. No training data needed, no cold start problem.

An ML layer sits on top: session co-occurrence embeddings (32-dimensional SVD vectors trained from listening sessions), temporal features (time of day, day of week), and a GradientBoosting classifier that learns selection patterns. The training pipeline, embedding generator, and predictor are implemented. The frequency model carries the system until enough sessions accumulate for meaningful model training.

Interface

35 tools, one conversation

The entire system is exposed through 35 MCP tools, the same Model Context Protocol pattern used in the task intelligence system. An AI assistant can add tracks to the library, log listens, trigger enrichment, run five independent discovery strategies, get recommendations with structured feedback, control Spotify playback, and query listening statistics, all within a single conversation. The assistant becomes a music librarian that understands the collection, remembers patterns, and can explain its reasoning.

This isn't a chatbot bolted onto a music player. It's a structured API layer that gives a general-purpose AI deep access to your personal music graph — broad intelligence orchestrating narrow, domain-specific tools.
