The Problem

Streaming algorithms are optimizing for the wrong thing

Spotify's Discover Weekly is optimized for engagement — keeping you listening — not for deepening your understanding of what you actually like. The algorithm feeds you adjacencies to what you've already consumed, reinforcing a narrowing corridor of taste. Over time, the bubble tightens. Artists you loved three years ago disappear from rotation. Genres you explored once never resurface. Your listening history exists only as a stream of play counts with no structure, no context, and no memory of why you were drawn to something.

The deeper problem is fragmentation. Your taste profile is scattered across half a dozen platforms — Spotify has play history, Last.fm has scrobbles, MusicBrainz has metadata, Discogs has pressings and lineage, Genius has lyrics and annotations. Each platform holds a shard of understanding, but none of them talk to each other, and none of them model the relationships between artists, genres, and tracks in a way that evolves with your behavior.

This project is built on a different premise: what if your music library was a knowledge graph that learned from how you listen?

The Architecture

Six APIs feed one unified schema

The system starts with Spotify as the canonical source — tracks, albums, artists, and playlists all keyed on Spotify IDs. From there, an enrichment pipeline fans out to five additional data sources, each contributing a different dimension of understanding. Last.fm adds listening history, scrobble patterns, and similarity networks. MusicBrainz provides authoritative metadata — ISRCs, recording relationships, and disambiguation. Deezer contributes audio previews and an independent recommendation graph. Discogs adds physical release data and artist lineage. Genius brings lyrics and annotations.

All of this collapses into a single normalized SQLite database — 27 tables in a relational schema designed so that any entity (track, artist, album, genre) can be enriched from any source without duplication. A token-bucket rate limiter manages API quotas across all six services, and the enrichment pipeline handles cross-referencing through external ID mapping.

The result is a graph where every track exists in context: its genre lineage, its artist's collaborators, its acoustic neighbors, its listening frequency, and its discovery provenance.

Genre Structure

Genres don't exist in isolation

Music genre taxonomies are notoriously messy. Spotify assigns genres to artists, not tracks. Last.fm uses crowd-sourced tags that drift over time. MusicBrainz has its own genre vocabulary. Rather than picking one canonical system, this project builds a co-occurrence network — genres are connected when they appear together on shared artists.

The result is a graph that captures real relationships: post-punk and darkwave cluster together. Math rock bridges prog and indie. Electronic music splinters into dozens of micro-communities connected by shared ancestry. The topology of this network reveals more about genre relationships than any hierarchical taxonomy could.

Node size reflects track count. Edge weight reflects co-occurrence frequency. Color groups genres by community detection. The network is interactive — drag nodes to explore neighborhoods, hover for details.

The Mechanism

Listening frequency evolves from behavior

Every track, artist, and genre in the system has a frequency — a target interval in days between listens. This isn't a static configuration. It's a living value that evolves based on your actual listening behavior, using the same golden-ratio mechanism from the task intelligence system.

Listen to a track ahead of schedule? Its frequency decreases — the system surfaces it more often. Let a track go overdue? Its frequency increases — the system lets it drift further into the background. The multiplier is the golden ratio: 1.382. Symmetric and bounded so cadence adapts quickly without becoming unstable.

Over time, this creates a natural rotation. Tracks you actively seek out earn tighter intervals. Tracks you passively ignore gradually fade. The system doesn't decide what you should listen to — it learns what you do listen to and adapts accordingly. Survival of the chosen.

Artist Network

Your library is a constellation

Every artist in the library exists in relationship to other artists. Last.fm's similarity data provides weighted edges — the stronger the similarity, the thicker the connection. When you add a new artist, the system maps them into this existing network, revealing which corners of your library they're closest to and which they're furthest from.

This isn't just visualization — it's the foundation of the discovery engine. When the system recommends new artists, it can explain why in terms of network proximity: "This artist sits between your post-rock cluster and your ambient cluster." The recommendation carries structural context, not just a match score.

Discovery

Recommendations from owned data, not rented algorithms

The discovery engine aggregates signals from the data sources you already feed the system. Last.fm provides similar-track and similar-artist recommendations anchored in scrobble data. Deezer contributes its own independent related-artist graph. ListenBrainz offers collaborative filtering from its open listening community. Genre exploration surfaces underexplored corners of your library's genre network.

Each discovery strategy runs independently, then results are merged and ranked by multi-source agreement. An artist recommended by both Last.fm similarity and Deezer related-artist graphs ranks higher than one from a single source. The system queues discoveries for review — you can add them to your library or dismiss them, and that feedback refines future recommendations.

The key difference from streaming platform recommendations: you own the graph. Discovery provenance is logged. You can see exactly why something was recommended, which sources agreed, and how it relates to what you already know.

Audio Analysis

Acoustic neighborhoods that transcend genre labels

Genre labels are human shorthand — useful but lossy. Two tracks labeled "electronic" can sound completely different. Two tracks from different genres can share deep acoustic properties: tempo, harmonic content, spectral texture, rhythmic density. Audio feature extraction captures what genre labels approximate.

The system uses Essentia for traditional audio feature extraction (tempo, key, mode, energy, danceability) from Deezer 30-second previews, and CLAP (Contrastive Language-Audio Pretraining) for 512-dimensional audio embeddings that capture holistic acoustic character. Cosine similarity in CLAP space finds tracks that sound alike regardless of how they're tagged.

Prediction

Learning what comes next

The ML layer builds on the same architecture pattern as the task intelligence system: co-occurrence embeddings capture track relationships, temporal features encode listening patterns, and a GradientBoosting classifier ranks candidates by likelihood of selection. The model learns from sessions — sequences of tracks listened to in proximity — and predicts what you're most likely to want next given the current context.

Before enough data accumulates for meaningful training, the system falls back to frequency-based ranking. Tracks with higher cycle progress (more overdue relative to their frequency) surface first. This means the system is useful from day one — the ML layer is an enhancement, not a prerequisite.

Interface

30 tools, one conversation

The entire system is exposed through 30 MCP tools — the same Model Context Protocol pattern used in the task intelligence system. An AI assistant can add tracks to the library, log listens, trigger enrichment, run discovery, get recommendations, and query listening statistics, all within a single conversation. The assistant becomes a music librarian that understands your collection, remembers your patterns, and can explain its reasoning.

This isn't a chatbot bolted onto a music player. It's a structured API layer that gives a general-purpose AI deep access to your personal music graph — the same architecture pattern where broad intelligence orchestrates narrow, domain-specific tools.
