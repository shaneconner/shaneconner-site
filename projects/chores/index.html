The Problem

Most task trackers are dumb lists

You check a box. Nothing learns. The system doesn't know that a barbell squat also worked your glutes, quads, lower back, and core. It doesn't know that completing a task early means you're ready for more challenge, or that consistently skipping something means it should back off.

What if a single logged action could update an entire knowledge graph? And what if the system could evolve its own scheduling based on your actual behavior?

The Insight

One action, rich data

Every exercise exists within a multi-dimensional taxonomy: muscle groups, movement patterns, equipment, energy systems, exercise types, and training objectives. When you log a single exercise, it recursively propagates through every parent category it belongs to.

The Mechanism

Survival of the fittest

Every task starts with the same frequency. Then the system watches what you actually do.

Complete a task early? The system multiplies the interval by the golden ratio (1.382×) — it loosens, making room for what matters more. Complete it late? The interval is divided by the same ratio — it tightens, because you clearly need more time between sessions.

Over months, frequencies evolve. Tasks you engage with regularly earn shorter cycles. Tasks you avoid drift toward longer intervals. Tasks you abandon entirely get naturally culled. No manual configuration required — the system shapes itself to your behavior.

Frequency Evolution Over Time

Synthetic data. Four exercises diverge from identical starting frequencies based on completion patterns. Adjustment points marked with circles.

The golden ratio (φ ≈ 1.618) appears throughout nature in growth patterns. Here, its reciprocal property (1.382 × 1/1.382 = 1) ensures symmetric, stable convergence — adjustments that are equally responsive in both directions.

Results

ML as a tool

The rich, multi-dimensional data model makes prediction almost trivial. A trained ML model predicts the next likely action with ~75% accuracy — not by being clever, but because the knowledge graph creates a feature space where patterns emerge naturally.

This points toward a larger idea: the future of AI isn't monolithic models doing everything. It's general intelligence (LLMs) orchestrating specialized tools — ML models, databases, APIs — each purpose-built for a narrow task. This prediction model is exactly that kind of tool: small, focused, and ready to be called by a larger system that understands context.
